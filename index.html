Let's start simple

What "data" do we handle in mutual aid work?

  • Names of people we serve and volunteers
  • Addresses for deliveries and pickups
  • Phone numbers and contact info
  • Needs assessments — who needs what
  • Distribution records — who got what, when
  • Communication logs — group chats, emails
  • Financial records — donations, expenses
  • Volunteer schedules and sign-ups

Why does this matter?

In recent years, government agencies have used organizations' own records as evidence against them. If you collect it, it can be requested, subpoenaed, or seized.

What is being proposed

New laws and investigations targeting organizations

The FENCE Act (S.497) — U.S. Senate, March 2025

Would make it a federal crime to shelter, transport, or financially help undocumented immigrants. Up to 5 years for the base offense, 10 years if the person has a prior conviction, 20 years if death or serious injury results. Critics say the language covers churches, homeless shelters, and mutual aid groups.

Congress.gov

Different approaches

Organizations that minimized data collection were more resilient

No More Deaths — Arizona

Desert aid camp. Raided 4 times by Border Patrol. 9 volunteers charged. Every prosecution failed.

They never kept formal intake records. When the government raided, there was no database to seize.

Hillsboro Mutual Aid — Oregon

~40 volunteers deliver food to ~60 immigrant families weekly. Their practices:

• Code names for all families • Encrypted messaging only • VPNs for communications • Printed directions, no GPS • No centralized database

4 Things to Remember

What can we do?

1. Only collect what you need

If you don't need someone's full name or address, don't ask for it. Less data = less risk.

2. Use encrypted tools

Unencrypted messages and files (email, SMS, Google Drive) can be read by the company and requested by authorities.

3. Control who has access

Check who can see your shared files. Remove people when they leave. Use strong passwords.

4. Choose tools with intention

If a tool wasn't built with your community's safety in mind, there may be a better option.
